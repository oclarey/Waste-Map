Instant Coffee Mix Bag

Made of several common materials such as...

  • Polypropylene (one of the most commonly used plastics).
    • Found in: clothing, piping, rigid plastic bottles, yarn / synthetic textiles, lunchboxes and food containers, autoparts, etc.
    • Produces microplastics that are carcinogenic.

The lamenant used in this packaging is unrecyclable by design.

Deflated Playground Ball

Made of synthetic materials such as...

  • Nylon (one of the most common synthetic fabrics).
    • Found in: Jackets, shirts, sweaters, pants, shoes, sportswear, hats, gloves, socks, sports equipment, fishing nets, tents, backs, rope, carpeting, upholstery, furniture, etc.
    • Makes up for 10% of ocean debris.
    • Can be repurposed and recycled by respinning and breaking down existing fibers. Companies like patagonia, Timbuk2, and Aquafil work repurposing nylon waste into new materials.
  • Polyester / Polyethylene (the most common plastic used today).
    • Breaks down in the sun and releases greenhouse gases.
    • Used in: liquid product containers, toys, fast food cups and packaging, utensils, polyester, shopping bags, garbage bins, etc.
    • Durable and recyclable, but only around 8% is recycled
  • Polyurethane.
    • Found in: tape, toys, paint/resins, shoes, sports equipment, industrial parts, rubber gloves, boots, wheels, automotive parts.
    • Multiple different methods of recycling: chemical and mechanical.
    • Most recycling is mechanical which is cheaper and results in a lower-value product Only around 10% or polyurethane is recycled.

Blue Fleece Fabric

Fleece is a polyester (polyethylene) based fabric...

  • Polyethylene (the most common plastic used today).
    • Breaks down in the sun and releases greenhouse gases.
    • Used in: liquid product containers, toys, fast food cups and packaging, utensils, polyester, shopping bags, garbage bins, etc.
    • Durable and recyclable, but only around 8% is recycled
    • Non-biodegradable and highly toxic.
    • High-density and low-density polyethylene can be recycled with the former theoretically able to be recycled 10 times at least.
    • Material must be clean and dry to be recycled.
    • Combinations of different plastics with different melting points in products makes recycling very difficult, as the materials are must be melted down and re-constructed. Food residue on packaging reduces recyclable material to the landfill.

The versatility of plastics like polyethylene is clear, but their life spans are harmfully being cut short.

Pill Blister Pack

Made of a blend of materials such as...

  • polyvinyl chloride (PVC): third-most widely produced synthetic.
    • Found in: piping, condiment packaging, drink bottles, toys, miscellaneous packaging, tubing, non-latex gloves, vinyl records, work boot components, etc.
    • Can be recycled through mechanical (grinded melted and moulded) or chemical (chemically separated and remade into different materials) processes.
    • Can be recycled, but only around 1% is recyclable because of cost and use of multi-material lamenants (such as this), which cannot be separated for recycling.
    • When used together in lamenants with other plastics and materials such as this, it is near impossible to recycle.
  • Polyamide (a plastic commonly used in clothing/textiles).
    • Breaks down in the sun and releases greenhouse gases.
    • Found in: jackets, leggings, sportswear, synthetic yarns / silks, etc.
    • Is recyclable and follows a similar process to nylon recycling.
    • Only about 18% is recycled.
  • Aluminum (100% infinitely recyclable).
    • Easily and cost effectively recyclable.
    • Recyclability is erased through multi-material lamenant.

Typically recyclable materials are designed into garbage through a lack of forethought.
